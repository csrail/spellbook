Arbitrary angle properties:\\
$~ ~$Applies to all $\mathbb{R}$.\\
$~ ~(x,y)$ is a point on the terminal side of the angle.\\
<br>
<br>
$$
\begin{aligned}
\begin{array}
$\displaystyle\sin{\theta} = \frac{y}{r} &
\displaystyle\csc{\theta} = \frac{r}{y} &
~~~ \Leftarrow y\ {\rm\text{relation}}
\\\\\\
\displaystyle\cos{\theta} = \frac{x}{r} &
\displaystyle\sec{\theta} = \frac{r}{x} &
~~~ \Leftarrow x\ {\rm\text{relation}}
\\\\\\
\displaystyle\tan{\theta} = \frac{y}{x} &
\displaystyle\cot{\theta} = \frac{x}{y} &
~~~~~~~~~ \Leftarrow y : x~~ {\rm\text{relation}}
\end{array}
\end{aligned}
$$

<br>
<br>

The trigonometric functions applied to any real angle results in a ratio between a pair of the values $x$, $y$ and $r$.  There is an abstraction in the co-ordinate system which maps the relationship of $\theta$ angle to $(x,y)$ co-ordinate. By the Pythagorean theorem $r$ will always be positive and $1$ since the magnitude of both $x$ and $y$ are always $1$.\\
<br>
$~ ~\displaystyle 0\pi \rightarrow (1,0)$
<br>
<br>
$~ ~\displaystyle \frac{\pi}{2} \rightarrow (0,1)$
<br>
<br>
$~ ~\displaystyle \pi \rightarrow (-1,0)$
<br>
<br>
$~ ~\displaystyle \frac{3}{2}\pi \rightarrow (0,-1)$

Then by using the trigonometric relations, we can observe whether a function is defined or undefined for a given $\theta$ angle. For example:

$~ ~\displaystyle\frac{\pi}{2} \rightarrow (x,y) = (0,1)$\\
$~ ~\displaystyle\Rightarrow \tan{\frac{\pi}{2}} = \frac{y}{x} = \frac{1}{0} = \text{undefined}$
<br>
<br>
$~ ~\displaystyle\pi \rightarrow (x,y) = (-1,0)$\\
$~ ~\displaystyle\Rightarrow \cot{\pi} = \frac{x}{y} = \frac{-1}{0} = \text{undefined}$

<br>
<br>
<br>

When the terminal angle $\theta$ is not acute, then it can be difficult to calculate the corresponding trigonometric relation. To manage this, find the co-terminal angle that is in between $0\pi$ and $2\pi$.  Then find the reference angle $\theta^\prime$ which is the closest acute angle to the x-axis.  The output of these transformations is an acute angle and its belonging quadrant that can then be used to calculate the trigonometric relations.

Example 1:\\
$~ ~\displaystyle\frac{21}{6}\pi$ is co-terminal with $\displaystyle\frac{3}{2}\pi$.  There isn't a reference angle because $\displaystyle\frac{3}{2}\pi$ is exactly on an axis $(0,-1)$.  Therefore $x=0,\ y=-1,\ r=1$.\\
<br>
<br>
$~ ~\displaystyle\frac{3}{2}\pi \rightarrow (x,y) = (0,-1)$
<br>
<br>
$\displaystyle\sin{\frac{3}{2}\pi} = \frac{y}{r} = \frac{-1}{1} = -1$
<br>
<br>
$\displaystyle\cos{\frac{3}{2}\pi} = \frac{x}{r} = \frac{0}{1} = 0$
<br>
<br>
$\displaystyle\tan{\frac{3}{2}\pi} = \frac{y}{x} = \frac{-1}{0} = \text{undefined}$
<br>
<br>

Note: $~\displaystyle-\frac{21}{6}\pi$ would be co-terminal with $\displaystyle\frac{\pi}{2}$, not $\displaystyle\frac{3}{2}\pi$\\
<br>
<br>
Example 2:\\
$\displaystyle \theta = -\frac{7}{6}\pi$\\
<br>
$\theta$ is co-terminal with $\displaystyle-\frac{7}{6}\pi + 2\pi \Rightarrow -\frac{7}{6}\pi + \frac{12}{6}\pi \Rightarrow \frac{5}{6}\pi \rightarrow \text{Q II}$\\
<br>
$\displaystyle\theta^\prime = \pi - \frac{5}{6}\pi = \frac{\pi}{6}$

<br>
<br>
<br>

$$\begin{aligned}
  \begin{matrix}
  \text{Quadrant II} & ~ & \text{Quadrant I}\\
  \sin{\theta}: + & ~ & \sin{\theta}: + \\
  \cos{\theta}: - & ~ & \cos{\theta}: + \\
  \tan{\theta}: - & ~ & \tan{\theta}: + \\
  ~~ & ~~ & ~~\\
  \text{Quadrant III} & ~ & \text{Quadrant IV}\\
  \sin{\theta}: - & ~ & \sin{\theta}: - \\
  \cos{\theta}: - & ~ & \cos{\theta}: + \\
  \tan{\theta}: + & ~ & \tan{\theta}: - \\
  \end{matrix}
\end{aligned}$$

<br>

$$\begin{aligned}
  \begin{matrix}
  \text{Quadrant II} & ~ & \text{Quadrant I}\\
  90^{\circ} < \theta < 180^{\circ} & ~ & 0^{\circ} < \theta < 90^{\circ}\\
  ~~ & ~~ & ~~\\
  \text{Quadrant III} & ~ & \text{Quadrant IV}\\
  180^{\circ} < \theta < 270^{\circ} & ~ & 270^{\circ} < \theta < 360^{\circ}\\
  \end{matrix}
\end{aligned}$$
